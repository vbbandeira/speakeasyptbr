📄 PAGE 04 — Ch. 1: Rhythm & Stress
Chapter 1

Rhythm
& Stress

Where the force falls

04

In English, stress is relatively free. In PT-BR, every word has a predictable stressed syllable — and getting it wrong makes you sound foreign even if every sound is correct.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
CAR-go (cargo)
→
ca-FÉ (café)
Stress falls later in PT-BR
HO-tel (hotel)
→
ho-TEL (hotel)
BRA-zil (Brazil)
→
Bra-SIL (Brasil)
🎧
Audio: 03_stress-demo_1.mp3, 04_stress-demo_2.mp3, 05_stress-demo_3.mp3 — slow then natural
The default rule
Words ending in -a, -e, -o → stress the second-to-last syllable
Words ending in other letters → stress the last syllable
Accents (á, é, ó, etc.) always override the rule.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
ca-FÉ
ho-TEL
Bra-SIL
Tu-DO bem?
VA-mos
co-MER
🎧
Audio: 06_rhythm_short-phrases.mp3, 07_rhythm_linking_1.mp3 — short phrases
⚠️ Common mistake
Don't stress the first syllable by default. 'CAfé' (English) → 'caFÉ' (PT-BR). The shift feels uncomfortable at first — lean into it.
📄 PAGE 05 — Ch. 2: Vowels
Chapter 2

Vowels

a / e / i / o / u + the traps

05

PT-BR vowels are purer and more consistent than English vowels. Each vowel has one sound — no diphthongs sneaking in. The tricky part is the open/closed distinction for E and O.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
'a' as in 'father'
→
a → casa, fala
no equivalent (closed e)
→
ê → você, leite
'e' as in 'bed'
→
é → pé, café
'o' as in 'so' (closed)
→
ô → avô, você
'o' as in 'off' (open)
→
ó → avó, pó
Grandma vs Grandpa!

English speakers add an 'uh' glide at the end of vowels. 'casa' → say 'CAsuh' → wrong. Keep vowels clean and short.

📄 PAGE 06 — Ch. 3: Nasal Sounds
Chapter 3

Nasal Sounds

ão · ãe · õe · em/en

06

Nasals are the most distinctively Brazilian sound. You literally feel them vibrate in your nose. English has nasal sounds too (m, n) — but not nasal vowels. This is new territory.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
no equivalent
→
ão → não, pão, mão
Nose + mouth + tail
no equivalent
→
em/en → bem, tem, também
no equivalent
→
ã → irmã, amanhã
Pure nasal
no equivalent
→
ões → limões, aviões
🎧
Audio: 20_nasal_ao.mp3 – 24_nasal_õe.mp3 — each nasal sound isolated
How to practise
Say "ão" — start with your mouth open, then close it slightly and let the sound move into your nose. Hold the vibration for a beat. You should feel your nose tingle.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
não
pão
mão
bem
também
amanhã
limões
então
🎧
Audio: 25_nasal_phrase_1.mp3 – 27_nasal_phrase_3.mp3 — Não tem pão · Eu também não
⚠️ Common mistake
Don't replace ão with 'ow' (like English 'now'). 'Não' ≠ 'now'. The nasal must resonate — it's a completely different exit point for the air.
📄 PAGE 07 — Ch. 4: R / RR / 'H'
Chapter 4

R / RR / 'H'

The sound English has no equivalent for

07

The Brazilian R is the sound most learners dread — and the one that makes the biggest difference. Once you get it, everything sounds more native instantly.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
'r' as in 'red'
→
r (single, middle) → caro, para
Soft tap, like 'd' in 'butter'
no equivalent
→
rr (double) → carro, correr
Guttural — from the throat
no equivalent
→
r at start → Rio, rua, rápido
Always guttural like 'rr'
h as in 'hot'
→
h words → hoje, hotel
Breathier, from the throat
🎧
Audio: 32_r_single_vs_rr.mp3 — caro vs carro — hear the difference
🎧
Audio: 33_r_word_start.mp3, 34_rr_inside.mp3, 35_h_sound.mp3
The guttural trick
Start with the English "h" in "hot." Now breathe a little harder and gargle slightly. That's the Brazilian RR. Words that start with R use this same sound.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
caro
carro
Rio
rua
correr
barato
hoje

🎧
Audio: 36_r_phrase_1.mp3 – 40_r_drill_words.mp3
⚠️ Common mistake
Don't roll the R like Spanish. PT-BR RR is guttural (throat), not rolled (tongue tip). They're completely different sounds.
📄 PAGE 08 — Ch. 5: T/D before 'i'
Chapter 5

T/D before 'i'

tia → 'CHia' · dia → 'JHia'

08

This is one of the most recognisably Brazilian features. Before the vowel "i", the sounds T and D shift into something that sounds like "ch" and "dj". Once you hear it, you'll never un-hear it.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
't' as in 'tea'
→
ti → tia, tinha, tipo
't' → soft 'ch' sound
'd' as in 'day'
→
di → dia, dinheiro, difícil
'd' → soft 'dj' sound
no shift
→
ta, to, tu → tudo, tá
Only before 'i'
🎧
Audio: 42_tia_dia.mp3 — tia vs dia — the key pair
🎧
Audio: 43_ti_di_words.mp3, 44_tch_dj_phrases_1.mp3 – 47_tch_dj_phrases_4.mp3
Why it happens
Before a high front vowel (i), the tongue naturally moves forward and the sound palatalises. It's not a "rule" you follow — it's physics of the mouth. Let it happen naturally.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
tia
dia
tinha
dinheiro
tipo
difícil
vinte
Bom dia!
🎧
Audio: 48_tch_dj_drill.mp3, 49_tch_dj_sentence.mp3
⚠️ Common mistake
Saying 'tia' with a hard English 't' ('TEA-a') sounds unnatural. The shift to 'chia' is subtle but essential — natives will notice immediately.
📄 PAGE 09 — Ch. 6: LH / NH
Chapter 6

LH / NH

filho · manhã · melhor · senhor

09

LH and NH are palatalized consonants — the tongue presses against the palate to create a softer, more musical sound. Think of "million" for LH and "onion" for NH.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
'll' in 'million'
→
lh → filho, velho, melhor
Tongue to palate
'ny' in 'canyon'
→
nh → banho, manhã, vinho
Nasal + palate
🎧
Audio: 50_lh_words.mp3, 51_nh_words.mp3
🎧
Audio: 52_lh_minimal.mp3, 53_nh_minimal.mp3 — mala vs malha · cano vs canho
LH practice
Say "million" — feel the tongue? Say it again, but only the "-llion" part. That's your LH.
NH practice
Say "onion" — the "ny" in the middle is your NH. Now say "manhã" with that same feel.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
filho
velho
mulher
melhor
banho
manhã
vinho
dinheiro
🎧
Audio: 54_lh_phrase.mp3 – 57_lh_nh_sentence.mp3
⚠️ Common mistake
Don't say 'FI-lyo' (like Spanish). The LH in PT-BR is one sound, not an L + Y combination. Keep the tongue pressed to the palate through the whole sound.
📄 PAGE 10 — Ch. 7: L final & S final
Chapter 7

L final &
S final

What English speakers get wrong most

10

Two sounds that trip up English speakers because they look familiar but behave very differently in PT-BR. Getting these right makes a big difference in fluency.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
'l' as in 'ball'
→
L final → Brasil, legal, sol
Sounds like a 'w' or 'u' glide
's' as in 'bus'
→
S final (before consonant) → mais, dois
Can sound like 'sh' in some words
'you' / 'cê'
→
você → cê (casual speech)
Major contraction
🎧
Audio: 58_l_final_words.mp3, 59_s_final_words.mp3
🎧
Audio: 60_deixa-eu.mp3, 61_voce_ce.mp3 — key reductions in natural speech
The final L secret
In PT-BR, a word-final L becomes a vowel-like "u" glide. "Brasil" → "Brazi-OO". "Legal" → "lega-OO". Don't say a crisp English L — let it dissolve.
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice
Brasil
mal
sol
legal
mais
depois
vocês
cê
🎧
Audio: 62_x_sound_words.mp3, 63_phrase_l_s_1.mp3 – 67_sentence_l_s_x.mp3
⚠️ Common mistake
Saying 'BraZILL' (hard English L) is one of the most common giveaways. The final L should dissolve — 'Brazi-OO' feels weird at first but sounds immediately more natural.
📄 PAGE 11 — Ch. 8: Connected Speech
Chapter 8

Connected
Speech

Linking words like a native

11

Natives don't say individual words — they say streams of sound. Syllables from one word flow into the next. If you pause between every word, you'll sound like a robot reading a script.

🇺🇸 English sound 🇧🇷 PT-BR sound
'Como é que' (3 words)
→
→ 'Coméki' (1 flow)
Words blur together
'Deixa eu ver' (3 words)
→
→ 'Dêchavér'
Natural reduction
'Você tá bem?' (3 words)
→
→ 'Cê tá bem?'
você → cê in casual speech
'Eu também não'
→
→ 'Eu tambémnão'
No gap between words
🎧
Audio: 07_rhythm_linking_1.mp3, 08_rhythm_linking_2.mp3, 09_rhythm_linking_3.mp3
The 3 linking rules
1.Vowel + vowel → they blend into one syllable
2.Final consonant + vowel → consonant moves to next syllable
3.Casual contractions: você → cê, está → tá, para → pra
🗣 Word drill — say each one twice

Deixa eu ver
Cê tá bem?
pra mim
tá tudo bem
vamo lá

The rule
If it sounds like too many separate sounds, you're not connected enough. Speed up slightly and let the words collide.
