## Lesson_01 — The 5 clean vowels

**Target:** a, e, i, o, u as pure single-shape sounds (no diphthongs, no glides)
**Duration target:** ~85s
**Stability:** 0.7 (max articulation for isolated vowels)

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 2. Brazilian Portuguese has five vowels: A, E, I, O, U. They sound clean and consistent. In English, vowels often glide — "say" is really *s-eh-ee*, and "go" is *g-oh-oo*. Your tongue moves mid-vowel. In PT-BR, each vowel is one static shape. You pick a position and hold it. Short, crisp, done.

Let's go through all five, slowly.

A — as in "father", but shorter. Listen: ah. ah. In the word "casa", house: CA-sa. CA-sa.

E — a clean, tense "eh". Listen: eh. eh. In the word "pé", foot: pé. pé.

I — like English "ee" but shorter. Listen: ee. ee. In the word "vida", life: VI-da. VI-da.

O — a pure "oh", no "oo" glide at the end. Listen: oh. oh. In the word "só", alone: só. só.

U — like English "oo" but shorter. Listen: oo. oo. In the word "tudo", everything: TU-do. TU-do.

Now repeat with me, all five in sequence: a... e... i... o... u.

And in words: casa... pé... vida... só... tudo.

Notice how each vowel ends abruptly — no tail, no glide. That crispness is what makes PT-BR sound clean.

## Lesson_02 — Open vs closed E

**Target:** é (open, "eh" as in "bed") vs ê (closed, tighter, tense)
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.7

### Script

Portuguese splits the letter E into two versions: open and closed. Mixing them up can change the meaning of a word. This lesson teaches you to hear the difference.

The closed E — written with a circumflex, like ê — is tight, tense, compact. Your jaw stays relatively closed. Listen: ê. ê. In the word "você", you: vo-CÊ. vo-CÊ.

The open E — written with an acute accent, like é — is wider, the jaw drops more, the sound is louder. Listen: é. é. In the word "pé", foot: pé. pé.

Hear them back-to-back: ê... é. ê... é. Closed... open. Closed... open.

Now in words. Closed: você... leite... três... mesa. Closed E throughout.

Open: pé... café... fé... festa. Open E throughout.

A classic minimal pair: sede, with closed E, means "thirst". SÊ-de. Séde, with open E, means "headquarters". SÉ-de. Same spelling, different sound, different meaning.

Drill it: sede... séde. sede... séde.

When you see an accent mark — the circumflex or the acute — it tells you which E to use. When there's no accent, you have to learn the word.

## Lesson_03 — Open vs closed O

**Target:** ó (open) vs ô (closed) — same principle as E, but with O
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.7

### Script

The same split happens with the letter O. Open O versus closed O. Let's train your ear.

Closed O — written with a circumflex, ô — is tight, your lips form a small circle. Listen: ô. ô. In the word "avô", grandfather: a-VÔ. a-VÔ.

Open O — written with an acute, ó — is wider, your mouth opens more. Listen: ó. ó. In the word "avó", grandmother: a-VÓ. a-VÓ.

Here's the most classic minimal pair in all of Brazilian Portuguese: avô means grandfather. Avó means grandmother. One sound flips the generation and the gender.

Drill them: avô... avó. avô... avó.

Another pair. Pôde — closed O — means "was able", past tense. PÔ-de. Pode — open O — means "can", present tense. PÓ-de.

Listen: pôde... pode. Pôde... pode.

In a sentence: Ontem ele pôde, hoje ele pode. Yesterday he was able, today he can.

If you mix these up, Brazilians will smile and correct you — it's one of those charming mistakes every learner makes. Drill the pair until you hear it without thinking.

## Lesson_04 — Unstressed vowel reductions

**Target:** Final -e → i, final -o → u in natural speech
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.5 (natural speed matters here)

### Script

In natural Brazilian speech, unstressed vowels at the end of words shift. It's subtle, but it's everywhere — and learning to hear it makes Brazilian Portuguese suddenly make sense.

Rule one: a final unstressed -e sounds like an "i". Listen to the word "leite", milk. Written with an E, pronounced: LEY-tchi. LEY-tchi.

Another: "noite", night. Written: n-o-i-t-e. Pronounced: NOY-tchi. NOY-tchi.

And "sete", seven: SÉ-tchi. SÉ-tchi.

Drill: leite... noite... sete. Notice the final "i" sound, even though it's written with an E.

Rule two: a final unstressed -o sounds like an "u". Listen to "muito", very: MUYN-tu. MUYN-tu.

Another: "tempo", time: TEM-pu. TEM-pu.

And "outro", another: OU-tru. OU-tru.

Drill: muito... tempo... outro.

These reductions aren't lazy speech — they're standard. Every Brazilian does it, in every register, from news anchors to kids on the playground. Learn to hear it, and your listening comprehension jumps forward fast.

## Lesson_05 — The "para → pra" reduction + minimal pair sentences

**Target:** The universal "para → pra" contraction in full sentences + review of open/closed
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

The word *para*, meaning "for" or "to", collapses to *pra* in conversation. Not slang. Not colloquial. Standard Brazilian speech, at every register except very formal writing.

Listen: Eu vou pra escola. I'm going to school. Pra, not para.

Another: Me dá uma pra levar. Give me one to go. Pra, not para.

And: Vamo pra praia? Let's go to the beach? Pra praia.

Drill with me: pra escola... pra levar... pra praia... pra mim.

Using "para" in everyday conversation sounds stiff, almost robotic. Say "pra" from day one.

Now let's combine everything from this chapter. Listen to these minimal-pair sentences that put the open/closed distinction to work.

Sentence one: O avô comprou o bolo. Grandpa bought the cake.

Sentence two: A avó comprou o bolo. Grandma bought the cake.

Same sentence structure. One sound — ô versus ó — tells you who did the buying.

One more: Ele pôde ir. He was able to go. Now: Ele pode ir. He can go.

Hear them side by side: Ele pôde ir... Ele pode ir.

You've now got the five clean vowels, the open/closed distinction, and the most common reductions. Chapter 3 tackles nasals — the sound that will instantly make you sound more Brazilian.
