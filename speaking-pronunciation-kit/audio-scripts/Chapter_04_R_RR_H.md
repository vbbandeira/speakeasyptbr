## Lesson_01 — H-sound orientation

**Target:** Start from English H, feel the throat, foundation for Brazilian R
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.7

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 4 — the Brazilian R. This is the sound most learners dread, and the one that transforms your accent the fastest when you get it. We'll build up to it gradually, starting from something you already know — the English H.

In Brazilian Portuguese, the letter H at the start of a word is completely silent on paper, but the sound it would make — a light breath from the throat — is exactly where your R will live.

Listen to these Brazilian words starting with H. The H itself is silent, but pay attention to the gentle throat friction. *hoje* — meaning "today". hoje. hoje.

Another: *hora* — meaning "hour". hora. hora.

And: *hotel* — meaning "hotel". hotel. hotel.

Drill: hoje... hora... hotel... humano... hábito.

Notice — no English "H-huh" at the start. The H is just a spelling placeholder. You pronounce it like the word starts with a vowel, but with a tiny breath of friction from your throat. That friction is your key to the Brazilian R.

Now try a sustained "ha" sound, like a quiet laugh. *hhhh-a*. Feel your throat — there's a soft scrape happening back there. Hold on to that sensation. That's the foundation for every R in this chapter.

## Lesson_02 — Initial R (guttural, same as H but stronger)

**Target:** Words starting with R — same throat position as H, more friction
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Now let's upgrade the throat breath into a full Brazilian R. When a Portuguese word starts with R, you use the exact same position as the English H — but with more airflow and friction. It's like a controlled gargle.

Listen to the name of the city: *Rio*. Rio. Rio.

Not "REE-oh" like an American would say — that curls the tongue back. The Brazilian *Rio* starts at the back of your throat, not at the tip of your tongue.

Another: *rua*, meaning "street". rua. rua.

And: *rápido*, meaning "fast". rápido. rápido.

Drill: Rio... rua... rápido... rato... roupa.

If you say the word *hoje* and then the word *Rio*, they should start in the same place — the back of your throat. The difference is just how hard you push the air.

Try the pair: hoje... Rio. hoje... Rio.

Now a trick. If you have trouble getting the guttural sound, pretend you're gently clearing your throat. Not a loud hack — just a quiet, controlled rasp. That's the consonant. Add a vowel right after it, and you've got a word.

Sentence: Eu moro no Rio. I live in Rio. Hear the guttural R at the start of Rio.

## Lesson_03 — Double RR (sustained guttural)

**Target:** RR between vowels — same guttural as initial R, held a bit longer
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

When you see double R inside a word, you use the exact same sound as initial R — a guttural scrape from the throat — but you sustain it a beat longer.

Listen to *carro*, meaning "car". carro. carro. Hear that heavy RR in the middle?

Another: *ferro*, meaning "iron". ferro. ferro.

And: *correr*, meaning "to run". correr. correr.

Drill: carro... ferro... correr... cachorro... terra.

Important warning: the Brazilian RR is *not* trilled like a Spanish or Italian R. If your tongue is vibrating at the tip of your mouth, you're doing the wrong sound. The tongue stays put. All the action happens at the back of your throat.

Spanish "perro" — tongue trill at the front.
Portuguese "ferro" — guttural friction at the back.

Hear them side by side isn't the point — just remember, *Brazilian RR never trills the tongue*. Your throat does all the work.

Sentence: O cachorro corre na terra. The dog runs on the earth. Four guttural Rs packed into one phrase. If your throat feels a little tired after practicing these, you're doing it right.

## Lesson_04 — Single R tap (completely different sound)

**Target:** Single R between vowels = a light tongue tap, NOT guttural — English "tt" in "butter"
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Here's the trap. When a single R sits between two vowels inside a word, it switches sounds entirely. It's no longer guttural. It's a quick tap of the tongue — almost exactly like the American English double-T in "butter" or "water".

Listen to *caro*, meaning "expensive". caro. caro. Hear that soft, quick R? It's a tap, not a scrape.

Another: *para*, meaning "for". para. para.

And the name *Maria*. Maria. Maria.

Drill: caro... para... Maria... era... barato.

These sound very American if you're used to pronouncing "butter" as "BUH-ter". The single R between vowels is exactly that flap of the tongue behind your top teeth. Quick, soft, gone.

Now the critical contrast. Listen to this pair:

caro — single R, soft tap — means "expensive".
carro — double R, guttural — means "car".

Listen again: caro... carro. caro... carro.

Completely different sounds. Completely different meanings. This one pair contains the whole secret of the Brazilian R: single R between vowels is a tongue tap, double R is a throat scrape.

Sentence with both: Eu quero um carro caro. I want an expensive car. Hear the gutural RR in *carro*, then the soft tap in *caro*.

## Lesson_05 — Minimal pair drill + full sentences

**Target:** Contrast training between single R and RR in minimal pairs + sentence integration
**Duration target:** ~90s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

To close this chapter, let's drill the minimal pairs that make or break your Brazilian R. Listen carefully to each pair. The meaning changes completely based on which R you use.

Pair one. caro... carro. caro means expensive. carro means car. caro... carro.

Pair two. coro... corro. coro is a choir. corro means "I run". coro... corro.

Pair three. moro... morro. moro means "I live". morro is a hill. moro... morro.

Pair four. fera... ferra. fera is a wild beast. ferra means "he brands with iron". fera... ferra.

Pair five. era... erra. era means "was". erra means "he misses" or "he errs". era... erra.

Drill all five again, back to back: caro, carro... coro, corro... moro, morro... fera, ferra... era, erra.

Now in sentences, at natural speed.

Eu quero um carro caro. I want an expensive car.

Vamos correr até o Rio. Let's run to Rio.

Hoje o cachorro do hotel fugiu. Today the hotel's dog ran away.

Que horror, corre, corre! How awful, run, run!

You now have the Brazilian R — the single most transformative sound in this kit. Chapter 5 is next, where we tackle the palatalization of T and D before the letter I.
