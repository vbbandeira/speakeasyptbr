## Lesson_01 — Ti drill (the "tch" shift)

**Target:** T before I becomes a soft "tch" — palatalization in action
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 5. One of the most instantly recognizable features of Brazilian Portuguese is what happens when T or D comes right before the letter I. The hard T becomes a soft "tch", like the start of the English word "cheese". This shift is called palatalization, and once you hear it, you can't un-hear it.

Let's start with T before I. The classic word: *tia*, meaning "aunt". Notice how it doesn't sound like English "TEE-a". It sounds like: TCHI-a. TCHI-a.

Another: *tipo*, meaning "type" or "kind". TCHI-po. TCHI-po.

And: *tinha*, meaning "had". TCHI-nha. TCHI-nha.

Drill: tia... tipo... tinha... time... gente... vinte.

Pay attention to *gente* and *vinte*. Those words end in E on paper, but remember — the final unstressed E sounds like an I. So the T before that I palatalizes. You get: GEN-tchi. VIN-tchi.

Listen again: gente. vinte. Both end in "tchi", not "tay".

This shift is automatic for Brazilians. They don't think about it. Your mouth will start doing it naturally too, once you've heard the pattern enough times.

## Lesson_02 — Di drill (the "dj" shift)

**Target:** D before I becomes a soft "dj" — same physics, different starting consonant
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

The same shift happens with the letter D. D before I turns into a soft "dj", like the start of the English word "jeep".

The classic word: *dia*, meaning "day". Not "DEE-a". It sounds like: DJI-a. DJI-a.

Another: *dinheiro*, meaning "money". di-NHEI-ro. Hear the DJ at the start: djee-NHEI-ru. djee-NHEI-ru.

And: *difícil*, meaning "difficult". DJI-fi-cil. DJI-fi-cil.

Drill: dia... dinheiro... difícil... pedido... Dinho.

Just like with T, this also applies when D is followed by an unstressed final E that sounds like I. Words like *pedido*, meaning "order" — pe-DJI-du. pe-DJI-du.

The key insight: palatalization happens because when you say the letter I, your tongue rises high and forward. The T or D right before it gets dragged along to that same position. The hard consonant becomes a soft one. It's not a rule you follow — it's physics of the mouth.

Once your tongue stops fighting it with an English hard D, the palatalization will feel automatic.

Sentence: Bom dia! Tudo bem? Good morning! All good? Listen to *dia* — djee-a. The palatalized D is the very first giveaway of a Brazilian accent.

## Lesson_03 — Minimal pairs and sentences

**Target:** Integration — when palatalization matters, real sentences at natural speed
**Duration target:** ~85s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

Let's put T and D before I together now, in real sentences.

A reminder: palatalization only happens before the letter I. It doesn't happen before A, O, or U. So *tudo*, meaning "everything", stays with a hard T — TOO-doo. And *data*, meaning "date", stays with a hard T — DA-ta. Only the I triggers the shift.

Compare: tia — with palatalized T — versus tudo — hard T. tia... tudo. Same letter T, two completely different sounds depending on what comes next.

Now a reminder about final E. It usually reduces to I, which means it triggers palatalization. Listen: *gente*, meaning "people". The T before the reduced I palatalizes: GEN-tchi. Not GEN-tay.

Drill the pattern: tia... tudo... dia... data... gente... gato.

Notice — *gato*, meaning "cat", has a hard T because it's followed by O. *Gente* has a soft T because it's followed by reduced I.

Sentences at natural speed.

Bom dia! Tudo bem? Good morning! All good? Hear *dia* palatalize, *tudo* stay hard.

Eu tinha dinheiro no tipo de conta errada. I had money in the wrong type of account.

A gente vai ser difícil, mas vai. We'll manage — it'll be hard, but we will. Three palatalizations: *gente*, *difícil*, and notice *vai* stays hard because no I.

Que difícil encontrar o tio do Tiago na festa de vinte anos. How hard it was to find Tiago's uncle at the twentieth-anniversary party. Count the palatalizations.

You've now nailed one of the most recognizably Brazilian features of the language. Chapter 6 handles the palatal consonants LH and NH.
