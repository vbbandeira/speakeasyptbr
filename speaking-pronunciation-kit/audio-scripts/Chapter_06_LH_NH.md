## Lesson_01 — LH family (the million sound)

**Target:** LH as a single palatal sound (not L+Y), same shape as English "lli" in "million"
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 6. Portuguese uses two letter pairs — LH and NH — that represent single sounds made by pressing the tongue flat against the roof of your mouth. These are called palatal consonants, and they give Portuguese its soft, melodic quality.

Let's start with LH. The trick is to think of the English word "million". Say it slowly: mil-lion. Pause on the middle: *lli*. Feel your tongue flatten against the roof of your mouth? That exact shape is your Brazilian LH.

Now Portuguese words. Listen to *filho*, meaning "son". FI-lho. FI-lho.

Another: *velho*, meaning "old". VEL-ho. VEL-ho.

And: *trabalho*, meaning "work". tra-BA-lho. tra-BA-lho.

Drill: filho... velho... mulher... melhor... trabalho... orgulho.

A critical warning for Spanish speakers: in Spanish, *hijo* is pronounced "EE-ho" or "EE-hyo", splitting into two sounds. In Brazilian Portuguese, *filho* is one single palatal sound. Your tongue doesn't break into two positions. It flattens against the palate and stays there through the whole LH.

Hear the difference. Spanish-style *hijo* — two moves of the tongue. Brazilian *filho* — one flat press.

*Filho*. FI-lho. Once.

Sentence: Meu filho trabalha muito. My son works a lot. Two LH sounds — *filho* and *trabalha*.

## Lesson_02 — NH family (the canyon sound, plus nasal)

**Target:** NH as a palatal consonant with nasal resonance — same position as LH + nose
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

NH is the cousin of LH. Same tongue position — flat against the roof of your mouth — but this time, air also flows through your nose. Think of the English word "canyon" or "onion". Say it: ca-nyon. Feel the middle? That "ny" is your Brazilian NH.

Portuguese words now. Listen to *banho*, meaning "bath". BA-nho. BA-nho.

Another: *manhã*, meaning "morning". ma-NHÃ. ma-NHÃ. Notice — this word is doubly nasal. The NH itself, and the final nasal "ã".

And: *vinho*, meaning "wine". VI-nho. VI-nho.

Drill: banho... manhã... vinho... dinheiro... cozinha... senhor.

Important: *dinheiro* contains both palatalizations from Chapter 5 and the NH sound. The D palatalizes into "dj" because of the following I, and then the NH kicks in. djee-NHEI-ru. djee-NHEI-ru.

A test — if you can say *senhor*, meaning "sir", without any English "N" consonant and with a clear palatal NH, your mouth has learned it. sen-HOR. sen-HOR.

Sentence: Meu senhor, um bom vinho na manhã de domingo. My sir, a good wine on a Sunday morning. Three palatals in one sentence.

## Lesson_03 — Minimal pairs and sentences

**Target:** LH/NH vs plain L/N + vowel — meaning-changing contrasts + natural sentences
**Duration target:** ~85s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

The fastest way to train your ear on LH and NH is through minimal pairs. These are words where the palatal sound is the only thing separating two different meanings.

Pair one: *mala* and *malha*. Mala is a suitcase. Malha is a knitted sweater or mesh. mala... malha. mala... malha.

Hear the difference? *Mala* has a plain L — MA-la. *Malha* has the palatal LH — MA-lha.

Pair two: *sono* and *sonho*. Sono means sleep. Sonho means dream. sono... sonho. sono... sonho.

Pair three: *cano* and *canho*. Cano is a pipe. Canho is a colloquial word for left-handed. cano... canho. cano... canho.

Pair four: *tela* and *telha*. Tela is a screen. Telha is a roof tile. tela... telha. tela... telha.

Pair five: *pena* and *penha*. Pena has multiple meanings — feather, pity, or sentence. Penha is a place name, also means cliff. pena... penha. pena... penha.

Drill all five pairs back to back: mala, malha... sono, sonho... cano, canho... tela, telha... pena, penha.

Now in full sentences, at natural speed.

Meu filho toma banho toda manhã antes do trabalho. My son takes a bath every morning before work. Count the LH and NH sounds — *filho*, *banho*, *manhã*, *trabalho*. Four palatals.

Minha mulher quer um vinho bom pra jantar. My wife wants a good wine for dinner.

O senhor pode esperar um minuto? Can you — formal "you" — wait a minute?

If you can get *trabalho* right — the LH at the end flat against your palate, no L+Y split — you've cracked the chapter. Move on to Chapter 7, where we cover final L, final S, and the casual *cê* reduction.
