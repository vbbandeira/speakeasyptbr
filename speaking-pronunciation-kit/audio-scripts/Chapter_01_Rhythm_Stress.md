## Lesson_01 — Stress rules: words ending in -a, -e, -o

**Target:** Default PT-BR stress rule (2nd-to-last syllable on words ending in -a/-e/-o)
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.6 (balanced — clear articulation + natural rhythm)

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 1 — Rhythm and Stress. In English, word stress is relatively free. In Brazilian Portuguese, it follows predictable rules. Let's start with the most common one.

When a word ends in the vowels *a*, *e*, or *o*, the stress falls on the second-to-last syllable. Let me show you.

Listen carefully: ba-NA-na. The stress is on "NA". Ba-NA-na.

Here's another: CA-sa. CA-sa. Two syllables — the stress is on the first.

One more: ES-co-la. ES-co-la. Three syllables — the stress lands on "ES".

Now let's do a quick drill. Repeat after me, out loud, feeling where the stress falls:

ba-NA-na... CA-sa... ca-DEI-ra... ES-co-la... JO-go.

In a full sentence: A ba-NA-na tá na ME-sa. The banana is on the table.

Notice how naturally the stress shifts to the second-to-last syllable. You don't need to force it — once you know the rule, your mouth will find it.

## Lesson_02 — Stress rules: other endings + accent overrides

**Target:** Words ending in consonants (stress on last) + accent marks override the default
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Now let's look at words that break the default rule. When a word ends in any letter *other* than -a, -e, or -o, the stress jumps to the last syllable.

Listen: ho-TEL. Ends in L — stress on the last syllable. Ho-TEL.

Another: Bra-SIL. Ends in L — Bra-SIL, stress on the last.

And: fa-LAR. Ends in R — fa-LAR. The stress is on the final syllable.

Drill with me: ho-TEL... Bra-SIL... fa-LAR... jor-NAL... co-MER.

Now the trump card — accent marks. When you see an acute accent like *é* or a circumflex like *ô*, the stress lands exactly where the accent is, no matter the word ending.

Listen: ca-FÉ. The accent is on the last *é*, so the stress goes there. Ca-FÉ.

Another: sa-bi-Á. Three syllables, and the accent on the final *á* pulls the stress all the way to the end.

And: LÁM-pa-da. The accent on the first *á* forces stress on the first syllable.

Drill: ca-FÉ... sa-bi-Á... ÁGUA... PÁS-sa-ro... LÁM-pa-da.

Remember: <strong>accents override everything</strong>. If you see one, the stress is there. No guessing.

## Lesson_03 — Linking and contractions

**Target:** Natural linking between words + casual contractions (você→cê, está→tá)
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.5 (natural conversational tone)

### Script

One of the most Brazilian things you can do is link words together instead of pausing between them. Listen carefully.

In textbooks, you see three words written separately: Como. É. Que. But in real conversation, Brazilians blend them into one flowing sound: Cóêki. Cóêki.

Try it with me, slowly first: Co... mo... é... que. Four beats.

Now at natural speed: Cóêki. One smooth blend.

Another everyday phrase — *Deixa eu ver*, "let me see". Separately: Deixa. Eu. Ver. Three words. But in speech, the vowels collide: Dêchavér. Dêchavér.

Your turn: Dêchavér.

And the most useful reduction of all — the word *você*, meaning "you", shortens to *cê* in conversation. And *está* becomes *tá*.

Formal: Você está bem? Are you well?

Casual — and this is what you'll actually hear: Cê tá bem? Cê tá bem?

Try the casual version: Cê tá bem?

These reductions aren't slang. They're standard Brazilian speech. Using the full forms sounds stiff.

## Lesson_04 — Full natural flow + stress minimal pairs

**Target:** Putting it all together — stress, rhythm, linking in full sentences + classic minimal pairs
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

Let's bring it all together now. Natural Brazilian Portuguese is a flow of syllable-timed rhythm with predictable stresses.

Listen to this sentence at full speed: Tudo bem? Cê tá indo pra escola?

Notice the rhythm — every syllable gets roughly equal time, with just the stressed syllables slightly longer. The words flow into each other.

Now, a quick lab — stress can change meaning in PT-BR. Listen to these pairs.

SÁ-bi-a. Stress on the first. It means "a wise woman".

sa-BI-a. Stress on the middle. It means "she knew".

sa-bi-Á. Stress on the last. It's the name of a Brazilian bird.

Three different words — spelled almost the same, but the stress tells you which one you mean. Let's hear the contrast again.

SÁ-bi-a... sa-BI-a... sa-bi-Á.

One more pair. DÚ-vi-da — stress on the first — means "doubt", the noun.

du-VI-da — stress on the middle — means "she doubts", the verb.

Mixing them up will confuse your listener. Rhythm and stress matter.

You now have the foundation. When you're ready, move on to Chapter 2, where we tackle the five Brazilian vowels and the open versus closed distinction.
