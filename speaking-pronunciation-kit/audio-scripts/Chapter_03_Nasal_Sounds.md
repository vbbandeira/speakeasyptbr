## Lesson_01 — Nasal orientation: feel the vibration

**Target:** Physical introduction to nasal vowels — what they feel like, how to produce them
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.7 (slow, deliberate articulation)

### Script

Welcome to Chapter 3. Nasal sounds are the most distinctively Brazilian feature of Portuguese. English has nasal consonants — M and N — but not nasal vowels. This chapter teaches your mouth and nose to work together.

Let's start with a physical exercise. Place your fingertips lightly on the bridge of your nose.

Now say the English word "no". N-O. You feel nothing on your fingers. All the air comes out through your mouth.

Now I'll say the Brazilian word "não", which also means "no" but is a completely different sound. Listen: nãããão. Feel it resonate — the vibration travels into the nasal cavity.

Try it: nãããão.

Brazilian nasal vowels are made by dropping the soft palate at the back of your mouth. Air exits through both your nose and your mouth at the same time. Your vocal cords vibrate, the nose amplifies, and you get that distinctive buzz.

Slowly: não. Hold it — nãããão. You should feel the buzz on your fingertips.

Now at natural speed: não. não. não.

If there's no vibration, the nasal isn't engaging. Exaggerate the "close" at the end of the vowel — bring your lips almost shut as you finish the sound.

This is the foundation. Every nasal in this chapter builds on this same feeling.

## Lesson_02 — The ão family and pure ã

**Target:** ão diphthong (not, bread, hand) + pure ã (sister, tomorrow)
**Duration target:** ~85s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

The most common nasal in Brazilian Portuguese is *ão*. It sounds like you're starting to say "ow" but then pull it into your nose. Listen.

não — meaning "no" or "not"... pão — meaning "bread"... mão — meaning "hand".

Drill: não... pão... mão. Feel the vibration in your nose on each one.

More ão words: João — the name John... então — meaning "so" or "then"... canção — meaning "song".

All of them: João... então... canção. Short, crisp, with the nasal buzz at the end.

Now a related sound — pure ã. Written with just the tilde on the A. No second vowel. The nasal hangs in the air.

Listen: irmã — meaning "sister". ir-MÃ. ir-MÃ.

Another: amanhã — meaning "tomorrow". a-ma-NHÃ. a-ma-NHÃ.

And: maçã — meaning "apple". ma-ÇÃ. ma-ÇÃ.

Drill: irmã... amanhã... maçã... lã... romã.

The difference between ão and pure ã is the trailing sound. Ão ends with a slight "w" after the nasal. Pure ã just holds the nasal and stops.

Compare: não... lã. não... lã. One glides into "w", the other just ends.

In a sentence: Amanhã eu vou com minha irmã. Tomorrow I'm going with my sister. Two pure nasals in one phrase.

## Lesson_03 — em, en, om, on — vowels carry the nasal

**Target:** How M and N at word endings nasalize the preceding vowel without sounding themselves
**Duration target:** ~80s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

When Brazilian Portuguese words end in *em*, *en*, *om*, or *on*, the M or N doesn't get pronounced as a consonant. Instead, it tells you to nasalize the vowel before it. This catches many English speakers off guard.

Listen to the word "bem", meaning "well". You might expect an English M at the end. Listen: bem. bem. No M sound. The vowel itself is nasal.

Another: tem, meaning "has" or "there is". tem. tem.

And: sem, meaning "without". sem. sem.

Drill: bem... tem... sem... também.

Notice — "também" ends in -bem with an M on paper. But you hear the nasal vowel, no consonant M.

Now the O-based versions. Listen to "bom", meaning "good". bom. bom. Once again — no English M at the end. Just the nasal O.

Another: som, meaning "sound". som. som.

Drill: bom... som... com... tom.

A common word that trips learners up: "também", meaning "also". Americans instinctively say "TAM-BEM" with a crisp M. But listen: tam-BEM. tam-BEM. Both syllables are nasal, no M consonant.

In a sentence: Eu também quero um bom som. I also want a good sound.

The pattern: M or N at a word ending — nasalize the vowel, don't pronounce the consonant.

## Lesson_04 — Nasal diphthongs: ãe and õe

**Target:** Two nasal vowels glued together (mãe, pães, limões, aviões)
**Duration target:** ~75s
**Stability:** 0.6

### Script

Some Brazilian words have two nasal vowels back to back. They form what's called a nasal diphthong — two nasal sounds, one syllable, no pause between them.

The first is *ãe*. Listen to "mãe", meaning "mother". mãe. mãe. You hear a nasal "a" gliding directly into a nasal "i" — all in one breath.

Another: pães, meaning "breads" — the plural of pão. pães. pães.

And: cães, meaning "dogs". cães. cães.

Drill: mãe... pães... cães.

Second diphthong: *õe*. Listen to "limões", meaning "lemons". li-MÕES. li-MÕES. A nasal "o" gliding into a nasal "i".

Another: aviões, meaning "airplanes". a-vi-ÕES. a-vi-ÕES.

And: canções, meaning "songs". can-ÇÕES. can-ÇÕES.

Drill: limões... aviões... canções.

Notice — these diphthongs always have the second nasal ending in that "i"-like buzz. Your lips start open, end nearly closed, and the whole thing vibrates through your nose.

A sentence tying it together: Minha mãe fez pães e cantou canções. My mom made breads and sang songs. Four nasals — try to feel each one buzz.

## Lesson_05 — Minimal pairs and full sentences

**Target:** Nasal vs oral minimal pairs (mão/mau, pão/pau, sim/si) + natural speed sentences
**Duration target:** ~85s
**Stability:** 0.5

### Script

To close this chapter, let's train your ear with minimal pairs. These are words that differ only by the nasal. Mixing them up changes meanings entirely.

Listen: mão — with a nasal — means "hand". Mau — without — means "bad". mão... mau. mão... mau.

Another: pão — nasal — means "bread". Pau — without — means "stick" or "wood". pão... pau. pão... pau.

And: lã — with a nasal — means "wool". Lá — without — means "there". lã... lá. lã... lá.

One more: sim — nasal — means "yes". Si — without — is the musical note "B" or a reflexive pronoun. sim... si. sim... si.

Drill all four pairs: mão, mau... pão, pau... lã, lá... sim, si.

Now let's put the nasals to work in real sentences, at natural speed.

Não tem pão. There's no bread. Two nasals — não and pão.

Eu também não. Me neither. Three nasals — eu também não.

Minha mãe comprou limões. My mom bought lemons. Four nasals packed into one phrase.

Amanhã vou com meu irmão. Tomorrow I'm going with my brother. Three more nasals.

If you can say these sentences with the buzz on every nasal, you've nailed the hardest sound category in Brazilian Portuguese. Move on to Chapter 4 when you're ready — we tackle the Brazilian R next.
