Rhythm

& Stress

Where the force falls

In English, stress is relatively free. In PT-BR, every word
has a predictable stressed syllable -- and getting it wrong
makes you sound foreign even if every sound is correct.

 ENGLISH SOUND                    PT-BR SOUND
                              ca-FÉ (café)
CAR-go (cargo)
                              ho-TEL (hotel)
Stress falls later in PT-BR   Bra-SIL (Brasil)

HO-tel (hotel)

BRA-zil (Brazil)

   Audio: 03_stress-demo_1.mp3, 04_stress-demo_2.mp3,

        05_stress-demo_3.mp3 -- slow then natural

The default rule

  Words ending in -a, -e, -o  stress the second-to-last
  syllable
  Words ending in other letters  stress the last syllable

   Accents (á, é, ó, etc.) always override the rule.

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

ca-FÉ      ho-TEL            Bra-SIL

Tu-DO bem?                   VA-mos   co-MER

 Audio: 06_rhythm_short-phrases.mp3,

     07_rhythm_linking_1.mp3 -- short phrases

 COMMON MISTAKE

Don't stress the first syllable by default. 'CAfé' (English)
 'caFÉ' (PT-BR). The shift feels uncomfortable at first --
lean into it.

Vowels

a / e / i / o / u + the traps

PT-BR vowels are purer and more consistent than English
vowels. Each vowel has one sound -- no diphthongs
sneaking in. The tricky part is the open/closed distinction
for E and O.

 ENGLISH SOUND                      PT-BR SOUND
                                a  casa, fala
'a' as in 'father'

no equivalent (closed e)  ę  vocę, leite

'e' as in 'bed'                 é  pé, café

'o' as in 'so' (closed)  ô  avô, vocę

'o' as in 'off' (open)          ó  avó, pó

Grandma vs Grandpa!

     vowel

 Audio: 15_minimal_pairs_e.mp3, 16_minimal_pairs_o.mp3 --

     avó vs avô -- train your ear

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

casa       vida                tudo   vocę   pé

avó        avô                 muito

 COMMON MISTAKE

English speakers add an 'uh' glide at the end of vowels.
'casa'  say 'CAsuh'  wrong. Keep vowels clean and
short.

Nasal Sounds

ăo · ăe · őe · em/en

Nasals are the most distinctively Brazilian sound. You
literally feel them vibrate in your nose. English has nasal
sounds too (m, n) -- but not nasal vowels. This is new
territory.

 ENGLISH SOUND                     PT-BR SOUND
                               ăo  năo, păo, măo
no equivalent
                                  em/en  bem, tem,
Nose + mouth + tail           

no equivalent                     também
                               ă  irmă, amanhă
no equivalent
                               őes  limőes, aviőes

no equivalent

     sound isolated

How to practise

Say "ăo" -- start with your mouth open, then close it
slightly and let the sound move into your nose. Hold the
vibration for a beat. You should feel your nose tingle.

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

năo        păo        măo     bem    também

amanhă                limőes  entăo

 Audio: 25_nasal_phrase_1.mp3 ­ 27_nasal_phrase_3.mp3 --

     Năo tem păo · Eu também năo

 COMMON MISTAKE

Don't replace ăo with 'ow' (like English 'now'). 'Năo' 
'now'. The nasal must resonate -- it's a completely
different exit point for the air.

R / RR / 'H'

The sound English has no equivalent for

The Brazilian R is the sound most learners dread -- and
the one that makes the biggest difference. Once you get
it, everything sounds more native instantly.

 ENGLISH SOUND                       PT-BR SOUND
                                    r (single, middle) 
'r' as in 'red'                  caro, para
                                    rr (double)  carro,
Soft tap, like 'd' in 'butter'   correr
                                    r at start  Rio, rua,
no equivalent                    rápido
                                 h words  hoje, hotel
Guttural -- from the throat

no equivalent

Always guttural like 'rr'

h as in 'hot'

Breathier, from the throat

     difference

The guttural trick

Start with the English "h" in "hot." Now breathe a little
harder and gargle slightly. That's the Brazilian RR. Words
that start with R use this same sound.

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

caro       carro                Rio    rua  correr

barato        hoje              hotel

 Audio: 36_r_phrase_1.mp3 ­ 40_r_drill_words.mp3

 COMMON MISTAKE

Don't roll the R like Spanish. PT-BR RR is guttural (throat),
not rolled (tongue tip). They're completely different
sounds.
CHAPTER 5                                                  08

T/D before 'i'

tia  'CHia' · dia  'JHia'

This is one of the most recognisably Brazilian features.
Before the vowel "i", the sounds T and D shift into
something that sounds like "ch" and "dj". Once you hear
it, you'll never un-hear it.

 ENGLISH SOUND                    PT-BR SOUND
                              ti  tia, tinha, tipo
't' as in 'tea'
                                 di  dia, dinheiro,
't'  soft 'ch' sound         

'd' as in 'day'                  difícil
                              ta, to, tu  tudo, tá
'd'  soft 'dj' sound

no shift

Only before 'i'

 Audio: 42_tia_dia.mp3 -- tia vs dia -- the key pair

 Audio: 43_ti_di_words.mp3, 44_tch_dj_phrases_1.mp3 ­

      47_tch_dj_phrases_4.mp3

Why it happens

Before a high front vowel (i), the tongue naturally moves
forward and the sound palatalises. It's not a "rule" you
follow -- it's physics of the mouth. Let it happen naturally.

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

tia        dia        tinha  dinheiro  tipo

difícil    vinte           Bom dia!

 Audio: 48_tch_dj_drill.mp3, 49_tch_dj_sentence.mp3

 COMMON MISTAKE

Saying 'tia' with a hard English 't' ('TEA-a') sounds
unnatural. The shift to 'chia' is subtle but essential --
natives will notice immediately.

LH / NH

filho · manhă · melhor · senhor

LH and NH are palatalized consonants -- the tongue
presses against the palate to create a softer, more musical
sound. Think of "million" for LH and "onion" for NH.

 ENGLISH SOUND                        PT-BR SOUND
                                     lh  filho, velho,
'll' in 'million'                 melhor

Tongue to palate                     nh  banho, manhă,
                                  vinho
'ny' in 'canyon'

 COMMON MISTAKE

Don't say 'FI-lyo' (like Spanish). The LH in PT-BR is one
sound, not an L + Y combination. Keep the tongue
pressed to the palate through the whole sound.

The final L secret

In PT-BR, a word-final L becomes a vowel-like "u" glide.
"Brasil"  "Brazi-OO". "Legal"  "lega-OO". Don't say a
crisp English L -- let it dissolve.

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

Brasil           mal            sol      legal  mais

depois             vocęs             cę

 Audio: 62_x_sound_words.mp3, 63_phrase_l_s_1.mp3 ­

      67_sentence_l_s_x.mp3

 COMMON MISTAKE

Saying 'BraZILL' (hard English L) is one of the most
common giveaways. The final L should dissolve -- 'Brazi-
OO' feels weird at first but sounds immediately more
natural.

Connected

Speech

Linking words like a native

Natives don't say individual words -- they say streams of
sound. Syllables from one word flow into the next. If you
pause between every word, you'll sound like a robot
reading a script.

 ENGLISH SOUND                PT-BR SOUND

'Como é que' (3 words)   'Coméki' (1 flow)

Words blur together

'Deixa eu ver' (3 words)   'Dęchavér'

Natural reduction

'Vocę tá bem?' (3 words)   'Cę tá bem?'

vocę  cę in casual speech

'Eu também năo'                'Eu tambémnăo'

No gap between words

 Audio: 07_rhythm_linking_1.mp3, 08_rhythm_linking_2.mp3,

      09_rhythm_linking_3.mp3

The 3 linking rules

1. Vowel + vowel  they blend into one syllable
2. Final consonant + vowel  consonant moves to next

   syllable
3. Casual contractions: vocę  cę, está  tá, para  pra

 WORD DRILL -- SAY EACH ONE TWICE

Como é que            Deixa eu ver  Cę tá bem?

pra mim     tá tudo bem             vamo lá

     survival phrases at natural speed

 THE RULE

      If it sounds like too many separate sounds, you're not
      connected enough. Speed up slightly and let the words
      collide.
